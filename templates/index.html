Process

From nucleus shape to a probability

The model reads the same contour descriptors a pathologist would note from a digitized slide, scales them, and passes them through a trained logistic regression boundary.

01

Describe the contour

Smoothness, compactness, concave points, symmetry, and fractal dimension — five ways of describing how irregular the nucleus boundary is.

02

Standardize and score

Each value is scaled and passed through a logistic regression trained on correlation-filtered, multicollinearity-checked inputs.

03

Read the probability

The output is a probability of malignancy, not a verdict — a starting point for a conversation with a clinician, never a replacement for one.

Input schema

What the model reads from each nucleus

Six contour and shape descriptors, each measured directly from the digitized fine needle aspirate.

smoothness_mean

Local variation in radius lengths along the boundary — how jittery the edge is, point to point.

0.053 – 0.163
compactness_mean

perimeter² / area − 1.0 — how tightly the boundary wraps its enclosed area.

0.019 – 0.345
concave points_mean

Count of concave portions cut into the contour — sharper dents tend to track malignant samples.

0.000 – 0.201
symmetry_mean

How closely the two halves of the nucleus mirror each other across its longest axis.

0.106 – 0.304
fractal_dimension_mean

"Coastline approximation" minus one — how much detail the boundary reveals as you zoom in.

0.050 – 0.097
symmetry_worst

The largest symmetry deviation found across the nucleus — its single most asymmetric reading.

0.156 – 0.664
